From the start of the DALBIT skincare line to the final confirmed vessel form, I made exactly 107 prototypes. At first the number felt like waste. Now I think those 107 iterations were the most important part of the entire project.

Prototype number one was beautiful. Perfect in digital rendering. The lit curves looked like moonlight. The proportions were right. The brand color fit. But when I printed it and held it in my hand — something was wrong. Too light. It slipped across my palm. It wobbled when set down. What you see on a screen and what you feel in your hand are completely different designs.

3D printing made this work possible. Before, making a single prototype meant sending out to a vendor and waiting days. Now I can revise an idea in the morning and hold the result by afternoon. Faster iteration made me bolder. Because I knew it was okay to be wrong. Being wrong stopped being a cost and became information.

## Saying Goodbye to the First Idea

The hardest part of prototype work is not technical. It's abandoning the first idea. I once held on to prototype number one far too long. Surely more time would improve it. Surely a little more refinement. Then somewhere past thirty I finally admitted: the form itself was wrong. I started over. From thirty-one, everything went in a completely different direction.

What's interesting is that the evolution from thirty-one onwards moved far faster. The failures from one through thirty had already built a body of knowledge: not this way. A prototype that goes in the wrong direction is still information. The key is exploring that direction quickly and learning from it quickly.

## Show Three, Know One

In client presentations, I always show three directions. Option A, B, C. But honestly, I already know which one is right. The other two exist to explain why that one is right.

I used to think this was dishonest. Now I see it differently. Showing three isn't giving the client choice — it's giving them context. You need A and B to see the value of C. Show only one with no conversation, and even if it's excellent, a lingering anxiety remains: what might have been possible?

Making 107 prototypes taught me this: conviction comes from repetition. If you try to find the right answer from the start, you can't make anything at all. Only when you stop fearing being wrong can you begin to approach being right.
