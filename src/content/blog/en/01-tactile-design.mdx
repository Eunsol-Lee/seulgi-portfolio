When I start a new design, the first thing I do is close my hand around nothing. In that empty space, I'm feeling for the right size — where my fingers land, the natural angle of my thumb. That physical intuition always comes before any measurement.

Cosmetics packaging in particular demands this. You need to reach for it half-asleep every morning, grip it while tears blur your vision as you remove makeup at night. There's no formula for how much weight is right, or how much force a cap should require. You learn it by opening and closing hundreds of samples until the answer lives in your hands.

During the DALBIT skincare line, I spent nearly a month doing nothing but touching prototypes. Every morning I'd pick them up without looking — feeling whether the cap sat right, whether the pump clicked with the right resistance, whether the cream jar could be opened one-handed without a fight.

A heavy glass jar feels luxurious — in theory. In practice, it's awkward on a shelf and nerve-wracking in a travel bag. Lightweight plastic is practical, but the moment it slips it feels cheap. The weight of a material is not just a matter of grams — it's a matter of trust.

On screen, everything is perfect. The light is always right, the angle always flattering. But an object that lives in the hand is different. One edge sitting wrong against the palm accumulates into daily irritation. That accumulation shapes how a person feels about a brand over time. I genuinely believe this.

So I still move my hands first, even at the concept stage. I make clay models before the CAD is finalized. I tape paper cups together to test lid heights. It looks ridiculous. But the difference between products that go through this and those that don't shows up clearly in the finished thing.
