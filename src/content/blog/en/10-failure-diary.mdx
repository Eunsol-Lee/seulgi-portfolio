A portfolio holds what worked. What the client was happy with, what the market responded to, what photographed beautifully. But what actually made me isn't there. The failures don't go into the portfolio, but they stay in my hands.

Years ago, I made a truly beautiful package. Thin edges, precise embossing, a special coating. When the sample arrived I could barely breathe. Then in actual shipping tests, every one of those thin edges crumpled. The structure concentrated impact at the corners in transit. Beautiful and functional are not the same thing. I learned that then.

Another failure. A bottle design the client absolutely loved. It was met with applause in the presentation room — perfect for the brand image, they said. Then we ran consumer testing, and the feedback was consistent: hard to hold. The middle was too narrow to grip with any strength, and it slipped when hands were wet. What a client loves and what a user can actually use are not always the same design.

## Learning to Set the Ego Down

After the second failure, I decided for a long time that nothing would be finalized without user testing. It was inconvenient. It stretched schedules. But there was something I needed more — the habit of confirming whether what I think is beautiful is actually right, verified not by my judgment but by real hands.

Ego is something a designer needs. Without self-conviction, you can't hold a direction. But when that ego starts blocking feedback, it becomes dangerous. "I'm right, they just don't understand it yet." I thought this once. It created the second failure.

## The Moment Failure Becomes Principle

Looking back through my working principles one by one, most came from failures. "Corner structure must pass an impact test" — from the shipping collapse. "Test grip when hands are wet" — from the slippery bottle. "Before any client presentation, show it to one non-expert first" — from multiple direction errors.

None of these principles are in any design textbook. They exist because I was wrong. So I feel indebted to those failures that never made it into the portfolio. Without them, the work I do now wouldn't exist.

Sharing failure as a designer is not easy. We're trained to show the finished, the beautiful, the successful. But I want to talk about what went wrong more often. It's a more honest portfolio, and it's the truer version of my story.
