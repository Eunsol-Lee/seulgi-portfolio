When I worked on the MOOD Lip Collection, the thing I spent the most time on wasn't the shades themselves — it was what to call them. Naming 12 colors isn't labeling. It's deciding what emotional frequency each shade will operate on.

"Nude Pink" and "First Light at Dawn" can be the same color. But the moment you pick them up, they feel different. The first one describes. The second one evokes. In cosmetics, a color name is a preview of the experience the product promises.

Korean, Japanese, and Western beauty markets have subtly different relationships with color. Korea tends toward clarity and translucency — colors that look like light on skin. Japan leans toward restrained sophistication — low-saturation, precisely modulated tones. Western markets absorb higher saturation and more confrontational color more readily. The same red lip requires a different calibration depending on the market it's for.

The harmony between packaging color and product color matters enormously. A deep burgundy lip in a white case creates a contrast that raises an entry barrier. Echo the shade family in the case design and the product reads as more cohesive, more premium. Color must have a conversation between what's inside and what holds it.

Photographing product color is its own problem. Reproducing accurate color on screen is nearly impossible. Every monitor renders differently. Every phone renders differently. Every lighting condition produces a different result. This is why the best cosmetics packaging chooses colors that sustain their intended emotional register under any light, not just the right light.

Color is not a physical wavelength — it's a cultural language. The same wavelength produces different emotions depending on context and culture. My job as a cosmetics designer is to speak that language fluently, and to own the consequences when I get it wrong.
