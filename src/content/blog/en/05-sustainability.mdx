"Eco-friendly packaging" is solidifying into a marketing term. Wrap something in one layer of recycled paper and it's sustainable. Use kraft-colored stock and it's natural. Surface gestures are receiving far more credit than their actual environmental contribution justifies. I find this uncomfortable.

Working on the HANEUL cushion compact was the first time I asked the question seriously: what does sustainable cosmetics packaging actually mean? Using recyclable materials? Reducing what gets thrown away in the first place? Building a system that can be used indefinitely with the right refill?

A refill system feels closest to a genuine answer. The environmental burden of cosmetics packaging sits mostly in the case, not the contents. Buy the case once, replace only the product — it's simple in theory. In practice: refills are often inconvenient, aren't distributed separately, or the case wears out faster than the contents anyway.

With HANEUL, I addressed all three. The refill mechanism — a quarter-turn at the base, no tools required — was verified through 200+ user tests. The case material, a recycled ABS and bio-resin blend, was developed for durability. And I held firm on one thing: the case design had to be beautiful enough that people would want to keep it longer than any refill.

There's a persistent belief that sustainability and luxury are in conflict. That eco means humble, and luxury means wasteful. I think this is wrong. The most sustainable thing is the thing you want to keep using. Packaging I don't want to take out of the drawer eventually doesn't get used at all. Packaging I actually want to hold gets used, and used again, and thrown away much later.

In a market saturated with greenwashing, designing for genuine sustainability is ultimately a question of honesty. Why each material was chosen. Where the limitations still are. How those limitations are being reduced. Being able to say those things clearly — that's what I think real sustainable design looks like.
