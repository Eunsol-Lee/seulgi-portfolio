When SOUL Perfume was first being conceived, the client said: "This is a fragrance for everyone — no gender." My first thought was: then the design has to be too. But I wasn't exactly sure what gender-neutral design meant. Whether it simply meant using grey or black. I didn't know then.

The cosmetics industry runs on old codes. Pink and pastels for women, black and navy for men. Rounded and slender forms read feminine, angular and heavy forms read masculine. These codes were invented. Someone decided them at some point, and time turned them into convention. They were never inherent.

For the SOUL project, I erased all of it. Before making any color decision, I asked myself: "If this color feels feminine or masculine, where does that feeling come from?" In almost every case the answer was: from having seen it that way in other cosmetics. Context assigned gender to the color. Not the product itself.

## Returning to Universal Design Principles

The answer to gender-neutral design is not new. It's a return to the principles of Universal Design. Hand size — grip comfort across a range of hand sizes. Weight — balance stable enough to hold securely in one hand. Opening — a mechanism that requires no significant force. These principles do not presuppose gender.

The SOUL bottle was ultimately completed in thick, clear glass. No color. No texture. Form: a palm-sized oval. Is this masculine or feminine? — The goal was a design that doesn't raise the question. After launch, the gender split among buyers was 46:54. Nearly equal.

## The Market Has Already Shifted

Global market research shows that gender-neutral beauty products have recorded double-digit annual growth for five consecutive years. Consumers in their twenties and early thirties are particularly fatigued by positioning that feels like it's for someone else. They want good ingredients, good form, good materials. They don't want a gender label in front of it.

This isn't a trend. It's design returning to where it was always supposed to be. Fitted to the human hand. Attuned to human senses. Focused on people, not on gender.

As a cosmetics designer, I'll keep asking this question. "Is this choice for people, or for convention?" When that question becomes habit, design can reach more people.
