Choosing a material is choosing a value system. Glass means weight, fragility, cold transparency. Plastic means versatility, lightness, and an environmental debt you have to be ready to explain. No material is neutral — every one carries a story.

Glass is honest. You can see the product. The weight communicates directly to the hand. The cold touch of it says: this is serious. That's why luxury skincare brands insist on glass — it delivers premium without a single word of copy. The catch is that it breaks. That small anxiety is also part of the experience, every time.

One common misconception: plastic doesn't mean cheap. Thick, matte-finished PETG can feel colder and smoother in the hand than glass. The issue is never the material itself — it's the wall thickness and the surface treatment. Cheaply made plastic is cheap. Plastic itself is not.

Bio-materials have arrived fast in the last few years. PLA derived from corn, bio-PE from sugarcane — the names sound clean but the physical properties still trail conventional materials. The gap is closing faster than I expected, and that's where I'm paying attention.

Working on the HANEUL cushion compact, developing a proprietary blend of recycled ABS and bio-resin, I confirmed firsthand that environmental responsibility and sensory quality don't have to be in conflict. The slightly rough-yet-smooth surface texture we landed on created a tactile quality that standard plastic simply can't offer.

Material selection should happen at the very start of a project, not the end. The moment you choose a material, the possible forms shift, the finishing options change, and ultimately the brand's voice changes. Material is not the output. It's the premise.
