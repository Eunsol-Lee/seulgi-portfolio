There's a moment when you show a finished design to a client for the first time. If their hand reaches out naturally, picks up the product — opens the lid without a word, closes it, opens it again — the design has succeeded. No explanation needed.

Imagine a cream jar. The lid opens with exactly the right resistance, releasing a faint whisper of air — the signal that the seal is perfect. The underside of the lid is smooth so cream doesn't catch on your fingers. The base is slightly weighted so when you set it down, it lands with quiet authority. Without a manual, your body already knows how this is used.

The opposite exists too. A perfume cap that requires three turns. A lotion pump where you can't tell if it's locked or open. A label so cluttered with product names and volumes that you can't find the front. These products inevitably demand explanation. Instructions get inserted in the box. The brand has to post tutorials on social media. Failing design is patched over with documentation.

## What Dieter Rams Left for Cosmetics

Of Dieter Rams' ten principles, the one I return to most often in cosmetics work is: "Good design is as little design as possible." Rams was talking about electronics, but the principle fits packaging even more precisely. Cosmetics are daily-use objects. Touched every morning, sitting on bathroom shelves, carried in handbags. Any unnecessary design in that space accumulates as daily fatigue.

But minimum doesn't simply mean less. Rams' minimum means only what's necessary, executed perfectly. Not an aesthetic of subtraction — an aesthetic of refinement. In a cosmetics vessel, this means a form that's comfortable to grip without a handle, a silhouette that communicates what the product is without a label, a weight that builds anticipation before you even open it.

## What's Invisible Is the Real Design

The best design work, when finished, looks as though there's no design at all. When a client doesn't say "I wish there was something more here," when a consumer picks up the product and just holds it instead of saying "it's pretty" — that's when design has done its job.

In the final stages of every project, I ask the same question: "Does removing this element create confusion about the product?" If the answer is no, the element goes. It feels sparse at first, and that causes anxiety. But with time, the certainty arrives that the space was right. Consumers are far more perceptive than we give them credit for. They feel before they understand.

So I keep removing things. Remove once more, then check whether what remains speaks for itself. If explanation is still needed, more hasn't been removed yet.
