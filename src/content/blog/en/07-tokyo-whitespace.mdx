When I walked into the Muji flagship in Ginza for the first time, something felt strange. Beautiful, but I couldn't immediately explain why. It took a while to understand: there was no explanation anywhere. No price signs. No product descriptions. No copy designed to catch your eye. Just the things themselves.

I hadn't gone to Tokyo to visit design studios. I just went, and ended up walking into stores. Muji Ginza, Shiseido The Ginza, Issey Miyake Aoyama. Three completely different brands, but the feeling I carried out of each was remarkably similar. A slowing of breath. The space not pushing me to hurry.

At Shiseido The Ginza, I spent more than thirty minutes looking at one skincare vessel. White glass, a single hair-thin logo. But holding it — cold, solid, heavy — produced a feeling that was precisely "luxury." Without a label, I felt more, not less. Because the brand said nothing, I had to feel it myself.

## Ma — Tension, Not Absence

Japanese aesthetics has a concept called ma. Simultaneously meaning space, interval, and pause, ma is not simply "empty." The moment an actor stops speaking on stage — that silence is ma. The gap between characters on paper — also ma. In cosmetics packaging, ma is the tension created by a surface without a label, a finish without texture, a material without color.

At Issey Miyake Aoyama, I experienced this directly through the perfume display. Bottles arranged on shelves at precise intervals — not too close, not too far. They didn't compete; they elevated each other. Each product existed independently while in conversation with the whole space. The placement itself was already design.

## Back in Seoul

Back in Seoul, I looked at my own work for a while. Things filled as the client had requested — ingredient badges, certification marks, award seals, volume markings, period-after-opening icons. Every element was individually necessary. Together, they left the product unable to breathe.

After that, I started separating conversations with clients into "legally required" and "nice to have." Required information gets consolidated to the back or bottom. The front is kept as empty as possible. Clients are always anxious at first. "Doesn't it look empty?" — Yes, it does. But that empty surface, in the hand, makes the product stay in memory longer.

Korean cosmetics design has a level of completeness found nowhere else in the world. The technology, the materials, the sensibility. But sometimes we try to show too much of that completeness. If Tokyo taught me anything, it's that knowing when not to show is also skill.
